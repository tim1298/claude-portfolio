Context

A nine-month "Shark Tank" for product

This was the capstone of Northwestern's Master of Product Design & Development Management (mpd²) program. Over nine months, professionals from different fields team up to design and pitch a disruptive product to a panel of judges and VC investors. It demands critical thinking, strategic planning, cross-functional collaboration, and persuasive communication — end to end.

Meet Elliot

Elliot couldn't breathe

"I can't breathe…"

Elliot reached for his babysitter as he gasped for air. He was four, had a severe peanut allergy, and was unintentionally given a Butterfinger. In the panic, his babysitter failed to administer an EpiPen on the way to the ER. He made it just in time. Luckily, he's now a bright eight-year-old. However, that day changed his parents forever.

Problem

How parents communicate care today — and why it breaks

Current solutions

Three workarounds — all inadequate

Printed instructions / unorganized

Overwhelming and inaccessible in an emergency. Parents print pages of care notes that caretakers can't find or read under pressure.

Digital documents / passive

Shared folders and texts are slightly more accessible but passive and unintuitive — caretakers don't follow through consistently.

Anxiety-driven calls / micro-managing

Real-time check-ins feel like micro-managing to the sitter, slowly eroding the long-term relationship parents depend on.

Pain points

What breaks in the handoff

Opportunity

One gap no existing app had filled

Every parent we interviewed described the same failure: when care changes hands, critical information gets lost, confirmation never arrives, and there's no trusted community safety net. We mapped the competitive landscape and found that every existing tool covered at most one of these three dimensions. Kidsync was designed to own all three — profile management, real-time task confirmation, and household-matched community — in a single mobile-first platform.

How it works

The product

Three capabilities map directly to the three things that broke in handoffs today: information that wasn't reliable, confirmation that didn't reach the parent, and a support network that didn't exist.

01

All-in-one care profile

Parents build a complete profile — conditions, medications, emergency contacts, and care tips. Authorized caretakers get everything at a glance, with media-rich medication guides they can follow under pressure instead of searching in an emergency.

02

Medication guides & instant document access

Step-by-step medication instructions with photos so a caretaker can act quickly even in a panic. The same profile gives instant access to medical history, insurance, and vaccination cards — the right document, immediately, when it matters most.

03

Interactive checklist with calendar

Caretakers check off time-based tasks and send real-time confirmation back to parents — closing the anxiety-driven communication loop without the parent having to chase updates.

04

Household matching & community

Kidsync connects households with similar care needs — matched on children's conditions, not just proximity — into a trusted network for support and babysitting, so parents stay connected and informed even when they can't be there.

Validation

Users wanted it before it existed

After presenting the concept to parents in usability walkthroughs, the response went beyond positive feedback — people asked when it would be available and said they'd pay for it immediately. These unprompted reactions were the strongest signal of product-market fit we received throughout the program.

Design process

From ambiguous problem space to a focused solution

The path from "childhood allergies" to a care-handoff platform wasn't obvious. It took primary research, rigorous synthesis, and several pivots before we landed on the moment where design had the most leverage.

Navigating ambiguity

We started broad — mapping the full problem space around childhood healthcare needs across Education, Prevention, Advocacy, and Treatments. The insight map revealed that most existing solutions addressed chronic management, but nothing addressed the acute handoff moment: the window when a child moves from one caretaker to another and critical information either transfers reliably or doesn't.

We used an opportunity map to evaluate concepts across desirability, feasibility, and viability. The lead concept — transferring care information during a handoff — sat at the intersection of all three and became the clear pivot point.

User identification

I approached user research with a mix of consumer segmentation, user personas, and journey maps to ensure the product addressed the correct issues for the right people. Three parent segments emerged — DIYers, Tell-Show-Doers, and Reaction Managers — so the product had to work for both the highly prepared parent and the one who only arms caretakers for emergencies.

Preliminary interviews

I defined the research objectives, scripted the interview guide, ran sessions via Dscout, and synthesized findings to validate assumptions, surface friction through product walkthroughs, and gauge interest and willingness to pay.

  • Key themes: proactive measures, peace of mind, effective communication
  • Key moments: care handoffs, emergencies when parents can't return in time, time-sensitive routines

Market research

We mapped the competitive landscape across three dimensions — real-time interactivity, shared access, and profile management — and found most apps covered only one or two. Kidsync was positioned as the first to integrate all three.

Development

I ran storyboarding sessions to explore use cases across different parent scenarios, then turned them into a prioritized user story backlog, a master user flow, and lo-fi wireframes that we tested and refined into high-fidelity screens.

Design challenges

Challenge 01

Refining the information hierarchy

The profile originally led with demographics and education, then care instructions, documents, and contacts. Research showed general care information matters most to babysitters in the moment, so we reordered the hierarchy and added media support for medication photos and video guides — putting life-saving instructions first.

Challenge 02

Prioritizing mobile accessibility

We first imagined Kidsync as a web app because of its complex information architecture. User feedback said a web app wasn't immediate enough — a panicked babysitter reaches for their phone, not a laptop. We pivoted to mobile-first.

Challenge 03

Enhancing data security

Some parents worried sitters might retain access after their duties ended. We added granular controls over the scope and duration of profile sharing — parents choose exactly which information categories a caretaker can see, and when their access expires automatically.

What it demonstrates

Kidsync took a complex, emotionally charged problem from primary research through segmentation, competitive analysis, and end-to-end product design — and arrived at a focused concept users wanted before it existed. The hardest call wasn't visual; it was narrowing a broad "childhood allergy" space down to the single handoff moment where design had the most leverage.

Next steps

Where Kidsync goes from here

The near-term focus is launching and scaling a B2C base around families of children with special healthcare needs. From there, the platform's architecture opens naturally to pet care and elderly care, and ultimately a B2B move — partnering with care facilities, schools, and hospitals to become a comprehensive care-management platform.
